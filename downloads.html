Introduction¶

The adoption of open access (OA) publishing is changing scholarly communication. Predicting the future prevelance of OA is crucial for many stakeholders making decisions now, including:

  • libraries deciding which journals to subscribe to and how much they should pay
  • institutions and funders deciding what mandates they should adopt, and the implications of existing mandates
  • scholarly publishers deciding when to flip their business models to OA
  • scholarly societies deciding how best to serve their members.

Despite how useful OA prediction would be, only a few studies have made an attempt to empirically predict open access rates. Lewis (2012) extrapolated the rate at which gold OA would replace subscription-based publishing using a simple log linear extrapolation of gold vs subscription market share. Antelman (2017) used one empirically-derived growth rate for green OA and another for all other kinds of OA combined. Both of these studies are based on relatively old data at this point (collected before 2012) and very simple models.

We propose a rich new approach based on a large-scale extrapolation of many different forms of OA (including gold, green, hybrid, and bronze OA), using the up-to-date Unpaywall dataset. The model will be used to predict the number of articles that will be OA over the next five years, and also to predict the proportion of scholarly article downloads that will lead readers to OA articles over time.

Future work will allow the model to predict the growth of Open Access for individual disciplines, journals, and article selections.

Data¶

The data used in this analysis comes from two sources: (1) the Unpaywall dataset and (2) the access logs of the Unpaywall web browser extension.

Past and current OA status: the Unpaywall dataset of OA availability¶

Predicting levels of open access publication in the future requires detailed, accurate data on open access now. This study uses the Unpaywall dataset on open access status. Unpaywall links more than 100 million research articles (everything with a Crossref DOI) to the Open Access URLs where the paper can be read for free. Unpaywall is built and maintained using data gathered from over 50,000 journals and open-access repositories from all over the world. The full Unpaywall dataset is freely, publicly available (see details: https://unpaywall.org/user-guides/research).

Relative download patterns: access logs of the Unpaywall web browser extension¶

Predicting the open access pattern of usage requests requires knowing the relative usage demands of papers based on their age and subject matter. This study has extracted these download patterns from the usage logs of the Unpaywall browser extension.

This extension is made by the same non-profit as the Unpaywall dataset described above, with the goal of helping people conveniently find free copies of research papers directly from their web browser. The extension has more than 200,000 active users and has received more than 50 million access requests between August 2018 and August 2019. The usage logs for Unpaywall are not made publicly available because of privacy concerns.

Methods and Results¶

The method presented here aims to provide a useful model of OA growth, taking the following complexities into account:

  • different forms of open access are being adopted at different rates
  • some forms of OA include a delay between when a paper is first published and when it is first freely available
  • future OA mandates will change behaviour
